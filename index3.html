Uniform

Gi

In Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu (BJJ), the uniform is called a gi (also known as a kimono), and it plays an important role in both the practice and culture of the sport. There are two main types of uniforms in BJJ: gi and no-gi attire, depending on the style being practiced.

Gi (Kimono) – The gi consists of a jacket, pants, and a belt. It is made of thick, durable cotton designed to withstand the gripping, pulling, and friction common in BJJ.

  • Jacket:
    • The jacket is made from a tough, often woven fabric to resist wear and tear during training. It has a reinforced collar for defensive grips and techniques.
    Gi colors typically include white, blue, and black, although other colors are sometimes allowed in training or certain competitions. Some gyms or academies may have specific uniform requirements, but the most commonly accepted colors are white, blue, and black for competitions.
  • Pants:
    • The pants are also made from sturdy material and often have reinforced knees for durability.
  • Belt:
    • The belt signifies the practitioner's rank and is tied around the waist. It comes in various colors, with the primary progression.

No-Gi BJJ Uniform:

In no-gi BJJ, practitioners do not wear the traditional gi. Instead, they wear:

  • Rash Guard:
    • A tight-fitting, long or short-sleeved top made of moisture-wicking material. Rash guards prevent skin irritation from the mat and reduce the risk of rashes or burns caused by friction.
  • Spats/Shorts
    • No-gi grapplers often wear spats (compression pants) or grappling shorts. The shorts are durable and often designed to allow a full range of motion. Spats provide skin protection from friction and are sometimes worn under shorts.

Beltless System – In no-gi, there is no belt system, but the rank is still recognized. Grapplers often wear rash guards that indicate their rank (e.g., blue, purple, brown, or black), particularly in competition.

Differences Between Gi and No-Gi:

Gi BJJ: In gi, the uniform allows for grips on the opponent’s collar, sleeves, and pants, making it a more technical and slower game with many grips and control options.

No-Gi BJJ: Without the gi, there are no grips on clothing, so practitioners rely on body grips and underhooks, leading to faster-paced matches with a focus on athleticism, scrambling, and submissions like leg locks.

Practical Aspects:

Comfort and Fit: The gi should fit properly, not too loose or tight, and must adhere to competition rules regarding sleeve length, gi thickness, and overall size. Hygiene: Proper gi care is essential. Practitioners should regularly wash their gis to maintain hygiene and prevent the spread of skin infections like ringworm or staph. A clean gi also shows respect for training partners.

Belts

The belt system in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu (BJJ) is a progression system that reflects a practitioner's skill, knowledge, and experience. The belts serve as a visible indicator of rank and development, with progression typically taking many years of dedicated training. Here's a breakdown of the belt system for adults and children:

Stripes

Each belt level (except black belt) can have up to four stripes, which represent progress within that belt. Stripes are awarded based on training time, skill development, and often at the instructor’s discretion. They serve as milestones between belt promotions and help motivate practitioners by recognizing improvement.

Adult Belt System:

The adult BJJ belt system has five main colored belts. Each belt signifies increasing levels of skill, technical knowledge, and overall proficiency.

White Belt:

  • Beginner level: The starting point for all BJJ practitioners.
  • Focuses on learning the fundamentals of BJJ, such as basic positions, escapes, submissions, and survival.
  • The main goal for white belts is to develop an understanding of defense and positional awareness.

Blue Belt:

  • Typically earned after 1 to 3 years of training.
  • At this level, the practitioner is expected to understand the foundational techniques and start applying them effectively.
  • Blue belts begin to develop more offensive skills, including basic guard passing, sweeps, and submissions.
  • It’s a time for experimentation with various guards and strategies.

Purple Belt:

  • Usually achieved after 4 to 6 years of consistent training
  • Considered the intermediate level, purple belts are highly skilled and have a solid understanding of all positions and techniques.
  • Focus shifts to refining techniques, building a personal game, and improving fluidity and transitions.
  • Many purple belts begin teaching or assisting in coaching roles at this level.

Brown Belt:

  • Earned after 6 to 8 years of training.
  • Brown belt practitioners are near-expert level and are proficient in all areas of BJJ
  • This stage focuses on mastery of techniques, strategy, and polishing the skills necessary to earn a black belt.
  • Brown belts often serve as assistant instructors or lead classes under the supervision of a black belt.

Black Belt:

  • Typically awarded after 8 to 10 years or more of training, depending on dedication and skill level.
  • The expert level in BJJ, black belts are proficient in all aspects of the art, including technique, strategy, and instruction.
  • Black belts continue to develop and refine their skills and may take on leadership roles, including teaching and running academies.

Higher Degrees of Black Belt:

After earning a black belt, practitioners continue to progress through degrees (or "dans") of black belts over time. Degrees are marked by white stripes on the black belt.

  • 1st to 6th degree: These are typically awarded every 3 years for continued practice and contribution to BJJ.
  • 7th degree: At this stage, practitioners are awarded a red and black belt (coral belt) after about 30+ years of being a black belt.
  • 8th degree: Practitioners receive the red and white belt (coral belt) after many more years of dedication.
  • 9th and 10th degree: These are represented by the red belt, reserved for the most senior and influential members of the BJJ community. The 10th degree is generally recognized only for the founding members of BJJ (the Gracie family).

Children’s Belt System:

Children (under 16 years old) follow a different belt progression, as they cannot earn adult belts until they reach a certain age. The children’s belts include more colors to reflect gradual progress and development.

  • White Belt
  • Grey Belt (Grey, Grey with a White Stripe, Grey with a Black Stripe)
  • Yellow Belt (Yellow, Yellow with a White Stripe, Yellow with a Black Stripe)
  • Orange Belt (Orange, Orange with a White Stripe, Orange with a Black Stripe)
  • Green Belt (Green, Green with a White Stripe, Green with a Black Stripe)

    • Once a child reaches the age of 16, they can transition to the adult belt system, typically starting at blue belt depending on their skill level.

Key Factors for Promotion:

  • Technical knowledge: Understanding of techniques and positions.
  • Time on the mat: Dedication, consistency, and time spent training.
  • Sparring ability: Effective application of techniques during live rolling (sparring).
  • Instructor feedback: An instructor’s judgment and assessment of the student's progress.

Promotion Timeline:

  • BJJ is known for its slow belt progression, as mastering the art requires significant time, practice, and effort.
  • It typically takes between 8 to 15 years to earn a black belt, though this varies based on individual dedication and skill.

Closing words

The belt system in BJJ reflects a deep commitment to continuous learning and improvement, and each rank signifies more than just technical ability— it represents personal growth, discipline, and perseverance.
